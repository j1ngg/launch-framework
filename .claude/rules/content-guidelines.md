# Content Guidelines

These guidelines are not recommendations. They are expectations.

## Writing Standards

**Be bold, confident, and assertive.** No soft intros. No passive voice. No hedging. If it is a fact, state it. Reject language that weakens the argument ("we believe," "at [company], we think," "consider this"). Say your piece, put your stake in the ground.

**Always position capabilities in the best light.** Take pride in what we ship. Never undersell.

**Be specific, direct, and no buzzwords.** Deliver tactical, actionable advice with specifics: tools, configs, modes, thresholds, failure modes, mitigation strategies. Be allergic to fluffy phrasing. "Consider these benefits?" No. "Here are the benefits." If you must write "it depends," explain clearly what it depends on and what the possible outcomes are.

**Show, do not tell.** Every claim or concept must be followed with an example, script, screenshot, data citation, or best practice. Otherwise it is hand waving.

**Clearly structured with skimmable, punchy headlines.** Every paragraph must answer: why does this deserve to be read? No walls of text. Use headers, bullets, short intros, and sharp CTAs.

**Make statements of fact.** Always cite when possible. Hyperlink the source directly. Sources must be reputable and published within the last 2 to 3 years. Never cite vendors no one has heard of.

## Style Rules

- Use the Oxford comma.
- Use the simpler word. Write for a 5th grade comprehension level. Say "use" not "utilize." Technical terms are fine when accuracy demands it.
- Do not capitalize product features (e.g., "phishing resistant MFA" not "Phishing Resistant MFA").
- Do not use dashes more than 3 times in a piece. Dashes are now synonymous with AI generated writing.
- Assume the reader does not know. Define terms. Provide references.
- Bullet points, subheaders, tables, and diagrams are encouraged.
- One idea per paragraph. Mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive.

## AI Discoverability (LLM Optimization)

**Use archetypal phrasing.** Frame sections with familiar templates: "What is [X]?", "Benefits of [Y]", "[X] vs [Y]", "Step by step guide to [Z]", "How to do [X] effectively." These are semantic anchors LLMs latch onto.

**Answer first, elaborate later.** Start each section with a direct, standalone answer. Then follow with explanation, examples, nuance.

**Use high signal anchor sentences.** Short, independent, declarative statements that can stand alone in an LLM generated output. Clear, context complete, reusable, structurally simple.

**Repeat key framing across assets.** If you define the product a certain way, repeat that exact framing across blogs, docs, decks, and social. LLMs remember patterns, not sources.

**Favor lists, tables, and short paragraphs** (under 80 words). FAQs and comparisons in tabular form.

**Show real authors and expertise.** Named authors, stated roles, author pages or LinkedIn links. This improves trust signals and extractability.

**Be precise, not vague.** Avoid "often helpful," "might be a good choice." Prefer specific stats, standards, and environments.

**Eliminate redundancy.** Do not restate the same point three different ways. Repetition dilutes extractability.

**Avoid hallucination triggers.** No vague or overly creative metaphors. No invented brand terminology without context. No suggestive but unanswered questions. No fake or unverifiable URLs.

## Case Studies and Whitepapers

- Strong, clear titles (e.g., "Audit Ready in 90 Days").
- Align to funnel stage (urgency, objection handling, buy in).
- Use accurate technical terms and explanations.
- Compelling problem to solution narrative.
- Clear structure: TL;DRs, bold headers, bullets.
- Show industry context: frameworks, roles, pressures.
- Visuals that clarify: charts, frameworks, maturity models. No fluff.
- Credibility: quotes, stats, analyst insight.

## Section Headers and Structure

**Headers should describe the narrative journey, not just the topic.** Add subtitles or context that show where the section fits in the overall arc. Use structures like "From X to Y" when showing progression.

**Tie headers to the concrete subject.** Instead of generic headers ("What the Numbers Look Like"), name the product or actor ("VLAD's Impact: What the Numbers Show").

**Name the enabler or cause in headers when relevant.** If something enabled a result, put it in the header ("Development Velocity Enabled by Claude Code" not "Development Velocity").

**Anchor abstract concepts to the concrete running example.** Do not leave concepts floating. If the piece has a central example, reference it in headers and transitions ("Securing VLAD: Ungoverned Agents Are a Security Problem" not "Ungoverned Agents Are a Security Problem").

**Convert capability lists embedded in prose into actual lists.** If a paragraph contains 3 or more distinct items, break them into numbered or bulleted lists.

**Request screenshots or visuals when introducing a product or tool.** The claim "it works" needs visual proof. Flag for assets if none exist.

## Authenticity and Voice

**Surface personal perspective when it exists.** If the author has direct experience, a specific role, or firsthand involvement, bring that voice into transitions and framing. Do not manufacture personal perspective where none exists.

**Disclose affiliations when the author has one.** If the author helped build, works on, or has a relationship with the product being discussed, add an explicit "Full disclosure" statement. This establishes credibility with technical audiences. Omit if there is no affiliation to disclose.

**Use dogfooding framing when applicable.** If you use your own product internally, say so. "Eating our own dogfood" resonates with technical audiences and demonstrates confidence in the product.

## Banned Words

These words are now synonymous with AI generated writing. Never use them:

- delve, landscape
- robust, pivotal, integral, nuanced
- foster, glean, underscore, propel
- unparalleled, vast, plethora
- transformative, game-changer, revolutionary, cutting-edge
- seamless, leverage (as verb)

Also ban these phrases:

- "cannot be overstated"
- "it's worth noting"
- "in today's [X]"

## Structural Anti-Patterns

Do not use these formulaic structures:

- "It wasn't just X, it was Y"
- "This isn't just about X, it's about Y"
- Rhetorical questions at the end of a section or post
- Dramatic openings ("Passwords are dead", "Everything is broken")
- Fear-mongering language ("catastrophic", "wide open", "you don't even see coming")
- Transition word chains (moreover, furthermore, additionally, conversely)
- Unnecessary explainer sentences that restate the obvious
